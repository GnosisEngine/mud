Chapter 01 — The Basics

Staking Your First Claim

Supply areas are dangerous places. Resources spawn continuously, which means players and NPCs flood in to extract them. Most people share the space uneasily, each taking what they find. You can do the same — but there's another option.

When you stand in an unclaimed room, you can declare ownership over it. From that moment, every extractor in your room who submits to you owes you a cut of what they take.

# Standing in Room 7, which is unclaimed
> claim 20
Room 7 claimed. Tax rate: 20%. Charter slot used: 1.

The number is your tax rate — a percentage of every resource yield extracted in this room by anyone who has submitted to you. The system collects it automatically, rounding up to the nearest whole number. You set it when you claim. You can claim different rooms at different rates.

Your claim is only as durable as your presence. The moment you go offline, a five-minute grace timer begins. If you don't return before it expires, every room you've claimed reverts to unclaimed. The timer starts the instant you log out — not after some delay.

Vulnerability

A rival who knows your schedule can simply wait for your grace timer to expire, walk into your rooms, and stake their own claims. Presence isn't just encouraged — it's the entire foundation of ownership.

A freshly claimed room generates nothing on its own. If nobody is extracting there, you earn nothing. The claim is a tax structure, not a mine — it only produces income when submitters are working.

Chapter 02 — Enforcement

The Threat

Claiming a room doesn't stop people from extracting in it. Anyone can walk in and take resources without submitting. They'll keep 100% of what they take — and you'll see none of it. The claim only matters to people who have agreed to honor it.

To create that agreement, you issue a threat.

# Bob is in your room, extracting without submitting
> threat bob 40
Threat issued to Bob. Submission duration: 40 min. Bob has 60 seconds to respond.

The number is the submission duration — how long Bob would be bound to your tax if he accepts. Maximum is 60 minutes. Bob now has one minute to decide.

Each outcome has a distinct shape:

Bob Submits

Bob is protected from your violence for 40 minutes. He extracts, pays your tax automatically, keeps the rest. You cannot attack him while the submission holds — but he can attack you.

Bob Fights

If Bob wins, you drop everything you're carrying and all your claimed rooms transfer to him — including any active submission income. If Bob loses, he drops everything. Your claims remain yours.

Bob Flees

Bob escapes without submitting. He keeps everything he extracted. You keep your claims. He can be chased — fleeing is a command in the combat queue, not an instant teleport.

Bob Ignores (timeout)

The threat expires after 60 seconds with no answer. You may re-issue it, or simply kill Bob — nothing prevents you from escalating to violence at this point.

You can only have one active threat per player at a time. But you can threaten as many different people simultaneously as you want — sweeping through your rooms issuing threats is a viable patrol strategy.

Chapter 03 — Scale

Expanding Your Territory

One claimed room produces modest income. The natural instinct is to claim more. You can — as many as you want — but each new room requires a charter slot, purchased with in-game currency. Charters have no hard ceiling. The limits that matter are softer and more dangerous.

> claim 25
Room 12 claimed. Tax rate: 25%. Charter slot used: 2.
> claim 15
Room 3 claimed. Tax rate: 15%. Charter slot used: 3.

Each room can carry a different tax rate. A dangerous, high-yield room might justify a higher rate. A quiet room in a low-traffic area might need a lower rate to attract extractors at all. The rates are a set of levers, not a single dial.

Each new room multiplies your income — but also multiplies your exposure. More rooms means more people inside your territory. More people means more potential challenges. You need to visit each room to stay aware of what's happening inside it. You cannot monitor everything from a single position.

The Patrol Problem

A claimant holding ten rooms cannot be in ten places at once. The rooms you aren't standing in are effectively unpatrolled — extractors work them without submitting, and you have no immediate way to know. Income is only realized when you enforce it.

This is where the system begins to exceed what one person can comfortably manage alone. The rooms scale linearly. The enforcement burden scales faster.

Chapter 04 — Aggression & Its Costs

The Vulture Chain

A common mistake among new claimants: treating violence as a clean solution. Someone won't submit. You escalate to combat. You win. Problem solved.

Supply areas are not empty. Other players are always nearby — extracting, watching, waiting. When you start a fight, you signal several things at once: your position, your health, your opponent's health, and the fact that your claims are in play.

A typical fight — observed

Alice — threatens Bob, escalates to combat
B
Bob — fights back, both taking damage
W
??? — standing nearby, waiting

The watcher doesn't need to be stronger than either combatant. They just need to be patient. Two players who have been fighting are never at full health. A third party who held back is. The math favors the observer.

The Chain

Watcher kills Alice → inherits all her claims. Bob, also weakened, becomes the next target. Watcher kills Bob → takes any resources Bob was carrying. The watcher fought once, at full strength, against exhausted opponents — and walked away with everything.

This is not an exploit. It is the intended equilibrium. The vulture chain exists specifically to put a cost on aggression. Before issuing a threat you intend to escalate, you must account for everyone else who can see the fight about to happen.

The strategic response is not to avoid combat — it's to fight when observers are absent or accounted for. A busy supply area at peak hours is a terrible place for a territorial dispute. A quiet corner room at an off hour is a different calculation entirely.

What This Changes

A threat is not just a question between you and one person. It's a public signal in a room full of people with their own interests. The decision to escalate is a read of the entire room, not just your opponent.

Chapter 05 — The Interior Threat

Warding Off Revolts

Your submitters are not your employees. They have submitted because the alternative — fighting or fleeing — cost more than paying your tax. That calculation is not fixed. It changes every time they look at the other submitters in your territory and ask: could we take her?

The answer is almost certainly yes. A single submitter attacking you violates their submission and marks them as a target — too costly. But ten submitters acting together is a different equation entirely.

The revolt is a prisoner's dilemma. Every individual submitter would be better off if the group revolts and succeeds. But any single person who defects — tipping you off, refusing to show up, or selling the plan — exposes everyone else and walks away clean.

Your Actual Defense

You don't need to be stronger than ten people. You need those ten people to not trust each other enough to coordinate. Keep submitters who don't know each other. Vary tax rates so their interests don't align. Make sure at least some of them have more to lose from your death than to gain.

There is also the penalty to consider. If a submitter attacks you and you both survive, their immunity is revoked permanently — and their tax obligation remains. They are now marked, vulnerable, and still paying you. A failed revolt attempt is one of the worst positions in the game.

The Asymmetry

Failed revolt: the attacker pays tax, can be killed on sight, and all other participants are exposed. Successful revolt: claims transfer to whoever lands the killing blow — not split among the group. Coordination is hard. The reward is concentrated. The downside is severe and personal.

The claimant who survives long-term is not the one with the highest tax rate. It's the one whose submitters have individually concluded that the current arrangement is better than the alternative.

Chapter 06 — Economic Weapons

Raiders for Hire

You don't always need to be present to apply pressure. NPC raiders can be contracted to descend on a rival's supply area — extracting resources, disrupting their submitters, and splitting the haul with you.

Raiders operate on contract terms you negotiate before they deploy. You specify the target area and your cut. They handle execution.

NPCs will not die for you. When combat becomes unfavorable, they flee. This is by design — they are not occupation forces, they are opportunists. A well-defended rival territory with active players present may repel the raid entirely and you receive nothing.

Strategic Uses

Raids are economic disruption tools, not conquest tools. They cannot claim rooms. They cannot hold territory. What they can do: drain a rival's resource buffer, thin out their submitter population, and keep them on the defensive while you focus elsewhere.

The hire mechanic also creates a proxy war layer. A powerful claimant who doesn't want to expose their own character in a contested area can fund raids that do the economic damage from a distance. The rival has to fight NPCs rather than track down the actual aggressor.

The Risk

A rival who can identify you as the hirer may retaliate directly — and you've just made an enemy without the protection of being present for the conflict. Hired raids are most effective when the hiring can't be traced, or when you're prepared for escalation.

Chapter 07 — The Full System

Building an Empire

At scale, one person cannot patrol ten rooms, monitor submitters for revolt signs, respond to threats, and guard against hired raids simultaneously. The solution is a group — but building one correctly is the hardest part of the claim system.

When a player joins your group, they automatically submit to you. They extract from your rooms, pay your tax, and receive a share of group tax income as wages. The distribution is negotiated on entry and can be customized per member.

The distribution mechanic means a productive empire genuinely benefits its members. The more rooms producing tax income, the larger the pool — and Carol's fixed percentage of a growing pool means her real earnings increase as you expand. Joining a powerful group isn't just submission, it's an investment.

The Loyalty Mechanism

Carol isn't defending you out of loyalty or fear. She's defending you because you dying collapses the income stream she depends on. Unless she's positioned to inherit the claims herself, your death is her financial loss. Entanglement creates alignment.

When you die to another player, claims transfer to the killer — but your group remains intact. They'll hunt the killer, retrieve the territory, and restore the system. This makes your group members' self-interest synonymous with your survival in a way that no threat or submission ever could.

The Internal Politics

Distribution shares are negotiated individually. If Carol learns that Dave receives a higher share for similar work, she has a reason to resent you or Dave — or begin quietly organizing the same revolt you've been managing among your external submitters. The empire's internal politics mirror its external ones.

The endgame of the claim system is not the most rooms or the highest tax rate. It's an organization whose members have individually calculated that the system continuing is worth more than whatever they could gain by tearing it down. Power through entanglement — not strength.
